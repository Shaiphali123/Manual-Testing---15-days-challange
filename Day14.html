Day 14 - Interview Q&A Compilation

Preparation is key to success. This compilation covers some of the most frequently asked questions in manual testing interviews. Understanding these concepts thoroughly will help you articulate your answers with clarity and confidence.

1. Basic Testing Concepts

Q: What is software testing?

A: Software testing is the process of evaluating and verifying that a software application does what it is supposed to do. The goal is to identify defects to ensure that the product meets the requirements and the end-user's needs.

Q: What is the difference between Verification and Validation?

A: Verification is checking if we are building the system right (i.e., does it meet the requirements and design specifications?). Validation is checking if we are building the right system (i.e., does it meet the customer's actual needs?).

Q: What are the different levels of testing?

A: The main levels are: Unit Testing (testing individual components), Integration Testing (testing combined parts of an application), System Testing (testing the complete and integrated software), and Acceptance Testing (validating the software against user needs).

2. Test Design & Planning

Q: What is a test case?

A: A test case is a set of conditions or variables under which a tester will determine whether a system under test satisfies requirements or works correctly. It typically includes steps, test data, expected results, and actual results.

Q: How do you write a good test case?

A: A good test case is clear, concise, and easy to understand. It should have a unique ID, a strong title, clear steps, specific expected results, and be reusable. It should focus on testing one specific thing.

Q: What is the difference between a Test Plan and a Test Strategy?

A: A Test Strategy is a high-level document that defines the overall approach to testing for a project or organization (e.g., what to test, which tools to use). A Test Plan is a detailed document that outlines the scope, objectives, resources, and schedule for testing a specific project.

3. Bug & Defect Management

Q: What is the difference between defect Severity and Priority?

A: Severity defines the impact a defect has on the application (e.g., critical, major, minor). It is set by the tester. Priority defines the urgency of fixing the defect (e.g., high, medium, low). It is usually set by the product manager or client.

Q: Can you explain the Defect Life Cycle?

A: The Defect Life Cycle is the sequence of stages a defect goes through. It typically starts as 'New', moves to 'Assigned', then 'Open'. Once fixed, it becomes 'Fixed'. The tester then verifies it, moving it to 'Re-tested' and finally 'Closed'. If not fixed, it can be 'Rejected' or 'Deferred'.

4. Test Execution & Reporting

Q: What are Entry and Exit Criteria in testing?

A: Entry Criteria are the prerequisite conditions that must be met before testing can begin (e.g., environment is ready, all required test data is available). Exit Criteria are the conditions that must be met before testing can be officially concluded (e.g., 95% of test cases passed, no open critical defects).

Q: What is a Test Closure Report?

A: A Test Closure Report is a formal document prepared at the end of the testing project. It summarizes all testing activities, final metrics (like test coverage and defect density), and provides a final assessment of the software's quality and readiness.

5. Scenario-Based Questions

Q: How would you test a simple login page?

A: I would test both positive and negative scenarios. Positive: valid username/password. Negative: invalid username/password, empty fields, password managers. I would also check UI elements, "Forgot Password" link, "Remember Me" functionality, and security aspects like password masking.

Q: You find a critical bug right before a release. What do you do?

A: I would immediately report the bug with a high severity and priority, providing clear steps to reproduce it. Then, I would communicate the finding directly to the test lead, project manager, and developers to ensure it's visible. The decision to delay the release would be made by the stakeholders based on the impact analysis.

5 Key Tips for Your Interview

  • Be Clear and Confident: Structure your answers logically. Start with a definition and then provide an example.
  • Use Real Examples: Whenever possible, relate your answers to projects you've worked on. This demonstrates practical experience.
  • Think Like a User: Show that you understand the business impact of bugs and the importance of user experience.
  • Know Your Fundamentals: Have a strong grasp of core concepts like the STLC, bug life cycle, and testing types.
  • Ask Questions: Show your interest by asking thoughtful questions about their testing process, team structure, or challenges.
