Day 12 - Exploratory & Ad-hoc Testing

Unscripted testing approaches such as Exploratory and Ad-hoc testing allow testers to think creatively, uncover hidden defects, and validate user experience beyond predefined test cases.

Exploratory Testing

Definition: Exploratory testing is a simultaneous process of test design, execution, and learning about the system.

Key Characteristics:

  • No predefined test scripts.
  • Relies on the testerâ€™s skill, domain knowledge, and intuition.
  • Helps discover critical issues quickly.

Practical Applications:

  • Early-stage testing when requirements are incomplete.
  • Identifying usability and user experience issues.
  • Testing complex workflows and new features.

Ad-hoc Testing

Definition: Ad-hoc testing is an informal, unstructured testing approach performed without planning or documentation.

Key Characteristics:

  • Performed without any test cases or documentation.
  • Tester relies purely on intuition and experience ("Error Guessing").
  • Often used to find defects missed in formal testing.

Practical Applications:

  • Quick checks after bug fixes (buddy testing).
  • Short testing cycles with limited time.
  • When formal exploratory sessions are not feasible.

Difference Between Exploratory and Ad-hoc Testing

Aspect Exploratory Testing Ad-hoc Testing
Approach Structured but unscripted (guided by charters) Completely unstructured and informal
Documentation Notes, session charters, and findings are maintained Generally, no documentation is created
Tester's Role Combines learning, test design, and execution Relies purely on intuition and past experience
Usage Early testing, usability, complex features Quick bug finding, short cycles, random checks

Best Practices for Unscripted Testing

  • In exploratory testing, use test charters to provide a clear goal and scope for each session.
  • Document key observations, defects found, and areas covered, even in informal sessions.
  • Use unscripted testing to complement formal, scripted testing, not replace it.
  • Focus on high-risk, critical, or newly developed areas of the application.

Interview Questions - Day 12

Q: What is exploratory testing?

A: It is a simultaneous process of learning the system, designing tests, and executing them on the fly, without predefined scripts.

Q: How is ad-hoc testing different from exploratory testing?

A: Exploratory testing is unscripted but still structured with goals and charters, and involves learning and documentation. Ad-hoc testing is completely unstructured, informal, and undocumented.

Q: When would you use exploratory testing?

A: It's best used during early development stages, when requirements are still evolving, for testing complex features, or to specifically identify usability issues.

Q: What are the advantages of ad-hoc testing?

A: Its main advantages are speed and flexibility. It can be performed quickly without any preparation and is effective at uncovering random defects missed by formal testing.

Q: Why are unscripted testing approaches important?

A: They are important because they leverage the tester's creativity and intuition to find defects that rigid scripts might miss. They provide a valuable supplement to formal testing, ensuring better overall coverage.
